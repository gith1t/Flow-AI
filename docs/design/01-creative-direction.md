# Creative direction

## Concept: The nocturnal research atelier

Yomirai is positioned as a place where scattered information becomes composed knowledge. Its Japanese influence should appear through discipline, balance, material sensitivity, and the treatment of empty space—not through costume.

The central visual metaphor is a contemporary research room after dark:

- blackened wood and lacquer-like surfaces;
- warm paper and off-white ink;
- vermilion marks used like editorial annotations or a personal seal;
- indigo for navigation, evidence, and depth;
- thin rules and carefully aligned columns;
- calm transitions that feel like sliding paper panels or rearranging notes on a desk.

This creates a distinctive identity while staying appropriate for a professional AI research tool.

## Japanese design principles

The terms below guide design decisions; they are not labels to display throughout the UI.

### Ma — intentional interval

`Ma` is the purposeful space between elements. In Yomirai it means:

- generous space around the primary research question;
- clear pauses between sections in long reports;
- separation between canonical workspace content and AI activity;
- fewer simultaneous borders, badges, and accent colors;
- animation pauses that make state changes comprehensible.

Empty space is functional. It establishes hierarchy and reduces cognitive load.

### Shibui — restrained sophistication

Premium quality comes from subtlety:

- deep near-black surfaces with small temperature differences;
- texture visible only at close inspection;
- one strong accent instead of many competing colors;
- high-quality type and alignment;
- controls that reveal detail on focus or hover rather than displaying everything at once.

### Wabi-sabi — material humanity

The digital interface remains exact, but it may include controlled imperfection:

- faint non-repeating grain;
- slight tonal variation in large atmospheric backgrounds;
- organic graph motion that settles into a precise layout;
- editorial underline or annotation marks with minimal irregularity.

Never reduce legibility or make data alignment imprecise in the name of texture.

### Kanso — purposeful simplicity

Every visible element earns its place. Secondary actions move into contextual menus; metadata appears progressively; important states remain explicit.

Simple does not mean empty. Research views can be dense, but their hierarchy must be calm and predictable.

## Brand personality

| Attribute | Expression |
| --- | --- |
| Intelligent | Clear information architecture and precise terminology |
| Quiet | Low-chroma surfaces, controlled contrast, minimal notification noise |
| Premium | Refined typography, subtle material effects, consistent motion |
| Trustworthy | Visible sources, revisions, evidence, and AI proposal boundaries |
| Futuristic | Adaptive visual views and fluid state changes, without sci-fi decoration |
| Japanese-inspired | Editorial space, material restraint, vermilion mark, indigo depth |

## Brand mark and wordmark

### Wordmark

Use lowercase `yomirai` as the primary wordmark. A custom wordmark can combine:

- geometric clarity in `yo`;
- slightly more open letterforms toward `mirai`, suggesting an unfolding future;
- one distinctive cut or join that can become the motion signature.

The wordmark should remain readable at 20–24 px height. Do not replace Latin letters with visually similar Japanese characters.

### Symbol

Potential direction: a minimal monogram formed from two offset frames or pages, creating a central path/node. It can suggest:

- layered knowledge;
- a portal into a research workspace;
- past evidence becoming future understanding.

A small vermilion square may act as a seal-like brand mark. It must be abstract and original; do not imitate a real family, institutional, or calligraphic seal.

### Motion signature

On launch or route transition, the symbol’s two planes align and reveal a narrow line of warm light. Duration: approximately 600 ms on marketing surfaces and 280 ms inside the product.

## Art direction

### Photography

If photography is used, prefer:

- macro paper, ink, dark wood, brushed metal, rain on glass, libraries, maps;
- architectural shadows and grids;
- desaturated urban night scenes with one warm or red focal point;
- human hands organizing, annotating, or comparing material.

Images should support research and craft, not tourism. Avoid generic temples, geisha imagery, lantern streets, Mount Fuji silhouettes, and cherry blossoms unless a specific story truly requires them.

### Illustration

Use abstract knowledge diagrams:

- thin luminous relations on a dark field;
- layered paper-like planes;
- topographic contours;
- editorial red marks and coordinates;
- cropped data fragments that resolve into a structured view.

Illustration line weight should match the product icon system. Avoid 3D cartoon characters and generic AI brains.

### Texture

Texture is atmospheric, not foreground content:

- monochrome grain at 1–2% opacity;
- faint paper fiber on warm light surfaces at 2–3%;
- subtle vertical tonal drift on hero backgrounds;
- no repeating high-contrast texture behind text or tables.

Ship texture as optimized assets or procedural CSS noise with a strict performance budget. Disable it in high-contrast mode.

## Composition

### Asymmetry

Use balanced asymmetry on marketing and overview screens:

- large title occupying 6–7 columns;
- product proof or graph extending beyond the central grid;
- a small vertical label anchoring the opposite edge;
- intentionally offset section headings.

Inside dense application views, alignment becomes more regular. Asymmetry should establish character, not make data harder to scan.

### Framing

Thin frames and corner marks can suggest research plates, archival folders, or print registration. Use them for hero demos, selected objects, and proposal boundaries. Never place a frame around every card.

### Vertical writing

Optional vertical Japanese or Latin micro-labels may appear only on spacious marketing sections or workspace covers. They must be real, reviewed copy—not decorative pseudo-language—and must not carry essential meaning.

## Voice and interface copy

Yomirai’s voice is calm, direct, and specific.

Prefer:

- “17 changes are ready for review.”
- “Three findings need stronger evidence.”
- “Research continues in the background.”
- “This source conflicts with two existing findings.”

Avoid:

- “Awesome! Your research is ready 🚀”
- “The AI is thinking...”
- anthropomorphic claims about hidden reasoning;
- mystical language about wisdom, Zen, or ancient knowledge;
- vague premium language without product evidence.

## Visual tension: future and archive

The design should deliberately combine:

- archival order: paper, annotation, bibliographic detail, stable object identity;
- future tooling: live graphs, AI proposals, semantic search, fluid view switching.

Neither side should dominate. Too much archive feels nostalgic; too much future becomes another neon AI dashboard.

## Anti-pattern checklist

- No red sun behind the logo or hero.
- No sakura particles as loading animation.
- No katana, torii, koi, wave, or anime motifs as generic decoration.
- No random Japanese words used as texture.
- No saturated red gradients across large surfaces.
- No excessive blur or translucent glass over dense data.
- No springy/bouncy transitions in professional workflows.
- No tiny low-contrast gray text justified as minimalism.
- No animated background competing with the research canvas.
